Romeo and Juliet

The Tragedy of William Shakespeare

Enter Verona

ACT I - PROLOGUE

Two households, both alike in dignity,

In fair Verona, where we lay our scene,

From ancient grudge break to new mutiny,

Where civil blood makes civil hands unclean.


From forth the fatal loins of these two foes

A pair of star-cross'd lovers take their life;

Whose misadventured piteous overthrows

Do with their death bury their parents' strife.


The fearful passage of their death-mark'd love,

And the continuance of their parents' rage,

Which, but their children's end, nought could remove,

Is now the two hours' traffic of our stage;


The which if you with patient ears attend,

What here shall miss, our toil shall strive to mend.

SCENE I. Verona. A public place.

Enter SAMPSON and GREGORY, of the house of Capulet, armed with swords and bucklers
SAMPSON Capulet
Gregory, o' my word, we'll not carry coals.
GREGORY Capulet
No, for then we should be colliers.
SAMPSON Capulet
I mean, an we be in choler, we'll draw.
GREGORY Capulet
Ay, while you live, draw your neck out o' the collar.
SAMPSON Capulet
I strike quickly, being moved.
GREGORY Capulet
But thou art not quickly moved to strike.
SAMPSON Capulet
A dog of the house of Montague moves me.
GREGORY Capulet
To move is to stir; and to be valiant is to stand: therefore, if thou art moved, thou runn'st away.
SAMPSON Capulet
A dog of that house shall move me to stand: I will take the wall of any man or maid of Montague's.
GREGORY Capulet
That shows thee a weak slave; for the weakest goes to the wall.
SAMPSON Capulet
True; and therefore women, being the weaker vessels, are ever thrust to the wall: therefore I will push Montague's men from the wall, and thrust his maids to the wall.
GREGORY Capulet
The quarrel is between our masters and us their men.
SAMPSON Capulet
'Tis all one, I will show myself a tyrant: when I have fought with the men, I will be cruel with the maids, and cut off their heads.
GREGORY Capulet
The heads of the maids?
SAMPSON Capulet
Ay, the heads of the maids, or their maidenheads; take it in what sense thou wilt.
GREGORY Capulet
They must take it in sense that feel it.
SAMPSON Capulet
Me they shall feel while I am able to stand: and 'tis known I am a pretty piece of flesh.
GREGORY Capulet
'Tis well thou art not fish; if thou hadst, thou hadst been poor John. Draw thy tool! here comes two of the house of the Montagues.
SAMPSON Capulet
My naked weapon is out: quarrel, I will back thee.
GREGORY Capulet
How! turn thy back and run?
SAMPSON Capulet
Fear me not.
GREGORY Capulet
No, marry; I fear thee!
SAMPSON Capulet
Let us take the law of our sides; let them begin.
GREGORY Capulet
I will frown as I pass by, and let them take it as they list.
SAMPSON Capulet
Nay, as they dare. I will bite my thumb at them; which is a disgrace to them, if they bear it.
Enter ABRAHAM and BALTHASAR
ABRAHAM Montague
Do you bite your thumb at us, sir?
SAMPSON Capulet
I do bite my thumb, sir.
ABRAHAM Montague
Do you bite your thumb at us, sir?
SAMPSON Capulet
[Aside to GREGORY]
Is the law of our side, if I say ay?
GREGORY Capulet
No.
SAMPSON Capulet
No, sir, I do not bite my thumb at you, sir, but I bite my thumb, sir.
GREGORY Capulet
Do you quarrel, sir?
ABRAHAM Montague
Quarrel sir! no, sir.
SAMPSON Capulet
If you do, sir, I am for you: I serve as good a man as you.
ABRAHAM Montague
No better.
SAMPSON Capulet
Well, sir.
GREGORY Capulet
Say 'better:' here comes one of my master's kinsmen.
SAMPSON Capulet
Yes, better, sir.
ABRAHAM Montague
You lie.
SAMPSON Capulet
Draw, if you be men. Gregory, remember thy swashing blow.
They fight
Enter BENVOLIO
BENVOLIO Montague
Part, fools! Put up your swords; you know not what you do.
Beats down their swords
Enter TYBALT
TYBALT Capulet
What, art thou drawn among these heartless hinds? Turn thee, Benvolio, look upon thy death.
BENVOLIO Montague
I do but keep the peace: put up thy sword, Or manage it to part these men with me.
TYBALT Capulet
What, drawn, and talk of peace! I hate the word, As I hate hell, all Montagues, and thee: Have at thee, coward!
They fight
Enter, several of both houses, who join the fray; then enter Citizens, with clubs
FIRST CITIZEN
Clubs, bills, and partisans! strike! beat them down! Down with the Capulets! down with the Montagues!
Enter CAPULET in his gown, and LADY CAPULET
CAPULET Capulet
What noise is this? Give me my long sword, ho!
LADY CAPULET Capulet
A crutch, a crutch! why call you for a sword?
CAPULET Capulet
My sword, I say! Old Montague is come, And flourishes his blade in spite of me.
Enter MONTAGUE and LADY MONTAGUE
MONTAGUE Montague
Thou villain Capulet,--Hold me not, let me go.
LADY MONTAGUE Montague
Thou shalt not stir a foot to seek a foe.
Enter PRINCE, with Attendants
PRINCE
Rebellious subjects, enemies to peace, Profaners of this neighbour-stained steel,-- Will they not hear? What, ho! you men, you beasts, That quench the fire of your pernicious rage With purple fountains issuing from your veins, On pain of torture, from those bloody hands Throw your mistemper'd weapons to the ground, And hear the sentence of your moved prince.

Three civil brawls, bred of an airy word, By thee, old Capulet, and Montague, Have thrice disturb'd the quiet of our streets, And made Verona's ancient citizens Cast by their grave beseeming ornaments, To wield old partisans, in hands as old, Canker'd with peace, to part your canker'd hate: If ever you disturb our streets again, Your lives shall pay the forfeit of the peace.

For this time, all the rest depart away: You Capulet; shall go along with me: And, Montague, come you this afternoon, To know our further pleasure in this case, To old Free-town, our common judgment-place. Once more, on pain of death, all men depart.
Exeunt all but MONTAGUE, LADY MONTAGUE, and BENVOLIO
MONTAGUE Montague
Who set this ancient quarrel new abroach? Speak, nephew, were you by when it began?
BENVOLIO Montague
Here were the servants of your adversary, And yours, close fighting ere I did approach: I drew to part them: in the instant came The fiery Tybalt, with his sword prepared, Which, as he breathed defiance to my ears, He swung about his head and cut the winds, Who nothing hurt withal hiss'd him in scorn: While we were interchanging thrusts and blows, Came more and more and fought on part and part, Till the prince came, who parted either part.
LADY MONTAGUE Montague
O, where is Romeo? saw you him to-day? Right glad I am he was not at this fray.
